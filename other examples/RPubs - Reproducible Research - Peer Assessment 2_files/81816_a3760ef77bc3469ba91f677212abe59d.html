Analysis of Events most Detrimental to Health and Economic Well-Being

Jessica

May 23, 2015

Synopsis

An analysis was performed of the U.S. National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration’s (NOAA) storm database. This database tracks characteristics of major storms and weather events in the United States, including when and where they occur, as well as estimates of any fatalities, injuries, and property damage. Tornadoes were found to be the most harmful events for population health, causing the most injuries and fatalities of any type of event. Tornadoes were also found to be the events most detrimental to economic well-being, causing the most combined property and crop damage.

Results

Most Harmful Events with respect to Population Health

The events most harmful with respect to population health are those that cause the greatest number of fatalities and injuries, as recorded in the “FATALITIES” and “INJURIES” columns of the data set.

# Calculate total number of fatalities and injuries caused by each type of event.
pophealth <- rawdata %>% select(EVTYPE, FATALITIES, INJURIES) %>% 
                         group_by(EVTYPE) %>%
                         summarise(fatalities=sum(FATALITIES), injuries=sum(INJURIES)) %>%
                         filter(fatalities + injuries > 0) %>%
                         arrange(desc(fatalities), desc(injuries))

# rearrange data frame so we can plot it
melted_pophealth <- melt(head(pophealth), id="EVTYPE")
names(melted_pophealth) <- c("EVTYPE", "outcome", "count")

ggplot(melted_pophealth, aes(EVTYPE, count)) + 
    geom_bar(stat="identity", aes(fill=outcome),) +
    labs(title="Figure 1: Most Dangerous Events for Population Health", x="Event type",
         y="Number of Outcomes") +
    theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 45, hjust = 1))

We conclude that tornadoes are the biggest threat to population health, with excessive heat the next most dangerous type of event.

Events with Greatest Economic Consequences

The events which have the greatest economic consequences are those that cause the most property damage and crop damage, as recorded in the columns PROPDMG and CROPDMG of the storm data set.

# Calculate total amount of property and crop damage caused by each type of event.
economicdamage <- rawdata %>% select(EVTYPE, PROPDMG, CROPDMG) %>% 
                              group_by(EVTYPE) %>%
                              summarise(propdmg=sum(PROPDMG), cropdmg=sum(CROPDMG)) %>%
                              filter(propdmg + cropdmg > 0) %>%
                              arrange(desc(propdmg), desc(cropdmg))

Whether sorted by property damage or crop damage, the top six event types are the same. The figure below shows the property and crop damage caused by the six types of events that cause the most property and crop damage.

# rearrange data frame so we can plot it
melted_econ <- melt(head(economicdamage), id="EVTYPE")
names(melted_econ) <- c("EVTYPE", "Damage", "count")

ggplot(melted_econ, aes(EVTYPE, count)) + 
    geom_bar(stat="identity", aes(fill=Damage),) +
    labs(title="Figure 2: Events Causing Most Economic Damage", x="Event type", 
         y="Amount of Damage in Dollars") + 
    scale_y_continuous(labels=comma) +
    theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 45, hjust = 1))

We conclude that hail causes the most crop damage, while tornadoes cause the most property damage and the most combined crop and property damage.

Future Work

This is not a very good analysis. It would probably be a better idea to take fatalities per year, and property damage per year, caused by various types of events. However, we believe this analysis to be sufficient for the purpose of practicing writing reproducible reports.
