Synopsis

In this report we aim to analyze the impact of severe weather events on public health and economy in the United States during the period 1950 to 2011. Data was obtained from the NOAA Storm database (https://d396qusza40orc.cloudfront.net/repdata%2Fdata%2FStormData.csv.bz2).

To investigate the impact on public health, the total fatalities and total injuries for each severe weather events was analyzed. We find that TORNADOES had the most adverse impact on public health.

To investigate the economic consequences, we compute the total economic damage caused by the severe weather events. The Crop Damage and Property Damage was combined together to get the total damage. We find that FLOODS cause the most economic damage.

The data for ‘EVTYPE’ seems messy. There are a total of 985 EVTYPES in the data and there seems to be repeats / spelling errors.

# Initialize a vector of tidy EVTYPES
f_events <- c("astronom", "avalan", "blizz", "flood", "wind chill", "debris", 
    "dense fog", "dense smoke", "drought", "dust devil", "dust", "heat", "frost", 
    "funnel", "freezing fog", "hail", "rain", "snow", "surf", "wind", "hurricane", 
    "typhoon", "ice storm", "lightning", "marine", "rip current", "seiche", 
    "sleet", "storm surge", "thunderstorm", "tornado", "tropical", "tsunami", 
    "volcanic", "waterspout", "wildfire", "winter storm", "winter weather")

# Convert the EVTYPES in the data to lower case and remove spaces 
x <- ds$EVTYPE
evtypes <- gsub(" ", "", tolower(x))

# Create a new tidy Event Type (EVTYPENEW)
for (i in f_events) evtypes[grepl(i, evtypes)] = i
ds$EVTYPENEW <- evtypes
  1. Transform the PROPDMG AND CROPDMG to dollar amounts by mapping the respective EXP field
# Map the PROPDMG to $ by mapping the PROPDMGEXP field
ds$PROPDMGEXP <- tolower(ds$PROPDMGEXP)
ds$PROPDMGEXP <- ifelse(ds$PROPDMGEXP == 'h', 100 , ifelse (ds$PROPDMGEXP == 'k', 1000, ifelse(ds$PROPDMGEXP == 'm', 1000000, ifelse(ds$PROPDMGEXP == 'b', 1000000000, 1))))
ds$PROPDMGDOLLAR <- ds$PROPDMG * ds$PROPDMGEXP

# Map the CROPDMG to $ by mapping the CROPDMGEXP field
ds$CROPDMGEXP <- tolower(ds$CROPDMGEXP)
ds$CROPDMGEXP <- ifelse(ds$CROPDMGEXP == 'h', 100 , ifelse (ds$CROPDMGEXP == 'k', 1000, ifelse(ds$CROPDMGEXP == 'm', 1000000, ifelse(ds$CROPDMGEXP == 'b', 1000000000, 1))))
ds$CROPDMGDOLLAR <- ds$CROPDMG * ds$CROPDMGEXP

#Create a new field totalling property and crop damage 

ds$TOTDMGDOLLAR <- ds$CROPDMGDOLLAR + ds$PROPDMGDOLLAR
  1. Other transformations like transforming date fields have been done
# Format the dates as dates
ds$BGN_DATE <- as.Date(ds$BGN_DATE, "%m/%d/%Y")
ds$END_DATE <- as.Date(ds$BGN_DATE, "%m/%d/%Y")

And thus an final tidy data set with 8 columns is obtained

Exploratory Analysis for Impact on Public Health

First we aggregate the ‘FATALITIES’ and ‘INJURIES’ by ‘EVTYPE’ and check the boxplot of FATALITIES.

#Aggregate FATALITIES and INJURIES by EVTYPE
dsiph <- aggregate (cbind(FATALITIES, INJURIES)~ EVTYPENEW, FUN=sum, data = ds, na.rm=TRUE)
boxplot (dsiph$FATALITIES, main ="Distribution of FATALITIES")

The boxplot for grouping of ‘FATALITIES’ by type of storm event shows concentration in few major event types as causes.

Hence we focus on top 20 event types for fatality and analyze them for impact of public health.

dsiph <- arrange(dsiph, desc(dsiph$FATALITIES))
n <-20
dsiph2 <- dsiph[1:n,]

The proportion of FATALITIES and INJURIES covered by this smaller data set is close to 95%.

This gives us confidence that the smaller data set is representative as far as impact on public health is concerned.

Results

Impact on Population Health

A total of 1.514510^{4} people have been killed and 1.4052810^{5} people have been injured in storm events in the USA in the analysis period.

Distribution of FATALITIES by Event Type

ggplot(data=dsiph2, aes(x=reorder(EVTYPENEW, FATALITIES), y=FATALITIES)) + geom_bar(stat="identity") + coord_flip() + xlab("") + ggtitle("Fatalities by Event Type")

Distribution of INJURIES by Event Type

ggplot(data=dsiph2, aes(x=reorder(EVTYPENEW, INJURIES), y=INJURIES)) + geom_bar(stat="identity") + coord_flip() + xlab("") + ggtitle("Injuries by Event Type")

Hence we can conclude that Tornadoes cause the most impact on population health among Storm Events.

Adverse Economic Consequences

A total damage of USD 4.764228510^{11} has been caused by storm events.

Distribution of economic consequences by Event Type

ggplot(data=dsec2, aes(x=reorder(EVTYPENEW, TOTDMGDOLLAR), y=TOTDMGDOLLAR)) + geom_bar(stat="identity") + coord_flip() + xlab("") + ggtitle("Economic Damage by Event Type")

Hence we can conclude that Floods cause the most economic damage among Storm Events.
